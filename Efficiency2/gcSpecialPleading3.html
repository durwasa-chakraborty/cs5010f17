Special Pleading

  • Real C/C++ programmers don't rely on malloc/free or new/delete. They write custom allocators instead.

Once upon a time, real programmers did their own register allocation and wrote their own storage overlays. Those tasks have been automated. Compilers now do a better job of register allocation than most programmers ever did. On general purpose machines that run more than one process at a time, virtual memory usually works better than anything an application programmer could write, because it can take the behavior of other processes into account.

For sufficiently simple programs such as our micro-benchmarks, it is not unusual for a custom allocator to outperform both garbage collection and non-custom explicit deallocation.

The custom allocator I wrote for the MPermNKL benchmark provides an example of this. That custom allocator/deallocator also accounts for over a hundred lines of code in a small program that, when written in Java, contains less than 250 lines of non-comment code. Furthermore, that custom allocator/deallocator is not an example of beautiful code. It is ugly, hard to understand, hard to debug, and hard to modify.

Garbage collectors are also notoriously hard to debug and to modify. Once you've written and debugged a garbage collector, however, hundreds of thousands of programmers can use it in millions of programs.
