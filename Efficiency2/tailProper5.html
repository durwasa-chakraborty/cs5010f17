Proper Tail Recursion is About Space Efficiency

The space efficiency of proper tail recursion is closely related to the following fact:

Garbage collection is asymptotically more space-efficient than stack allocation.

Some programmers may be surprised by that, but the reason for it is easy to understand.

With any reasonable garbage collector, the amount of memory devoted to objects that are unreachable is always bounded by a small multiple of the memory devoted to objects that are reachable.

When an object is allocated on a stack, it will remain allocated so long as the stack frame remains allocated, even after there are no more references to the object. With stack allocation of objects, it is easy to construct examples for which the memory occupied by objects on the stack grows without bound while the memory occupied by reachable objects remains constant.

The worst-case space efficiency of stack allocation is therefore asymptotically worse than the worst-case space efficiency of garbage collection.

In like manner, the worst-case space efficiency of implementations that are not properly tail recursive is asymptotically worse than the worst-case space efficiency of otherwise identical but properly tail recursive implementations. We have already given an example of that, and the general theorem is not too hard to prove mathematically.
